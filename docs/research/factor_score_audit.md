# ETF-GAP-008A Factor Score Audit

ETF-GAP-008A audits the configurable factor score reports generated by ETF-GAP-008. It is an explanation and coverage layer only. It does not replace `compare_signal`, does not connect to the existing candidate strategy, does not change backtest returns, and does not change the UI.

## What The Current Score Can Explain

`output/factor_score_report.csv` can explain which ETFs received a computable multi-factor score under the current configuration, and `output/factor_score_detail.csv` explains which configured factors were actually used, skipped, unavailable, or disabled for each ETF.

For the current 50-symbol scoring universe, 24 ETFs are scoreable and 26 ETFs are not scoreable. The scoreable ETFs are mainly supported by these factors:

- `max_drawdown_60d`
- `avg_amount_20`
- `data_completeness`
- `volatility_20d`
- `momentum_20d`
- `momentum_60d`

The `total_score` is a weighted average of `factor_status=used` rows only. Missing optional values are skipped and are not filled with zero.

## What The Current Score Cannot Explain

The current score cannot explain benchmark-relative performance, tracking quality, premium/discount, fund size, or fee competitiveness. Those inputs are either unavailable from current sources or intentionally disabled.

It also cannot be treated as a final recommendation pool. The scoreable subset is an output coverage subset, not an investable candidate list. A symbol can be scoreable because a few price-derived fields exist, while still failing broader data quality checks such as short price history.

## Why Tracking Error And Discount Premium Are Excluded

`tracking_error` and `relative_return_60d` require confirmed benchmark mappings and schema-valid index cache. If benchmark data is missing, a price-only ETF series cannot produce a valid benchmark-relative metric. Treating missing benchmark metrics as zero would falsely reward ETFs whose benchmark dependency is simply absent.

`discount_premium` requires NAV or IOPV. Exchange price alone only gives market price movement; it does not reveal whether the ETF traded above or below fund value. Until NAV or IOPV data exists, discount/premium must remain `source_unavailable` and outside the score.

## Why 24 Scoreable ETFs Are Not A Recommendation Pool

The 24 scoreable ETFs only indicate that at least one enabled factor produced a usable numeric contribution. They are not automatically better than the 26 unscoreable ETFs, because missing sources are unevenly distributed.

Current scoreability is heavily shaped by strategy-signal and indicator-cache availability. Benchmark-dependent and NAV/IOPV-dependent factors do not participate at all, and metadata factors such as `fund_size` and `management_fee` are disabled because source coverage is not confirmed. That means the score is a partial explanation, not a complete ETF selection model.

## Data Completeness Bias

`data_completeness` currently participates as a factor for scoreable ETFs. This is useful as a diagnostic signal, but it can also introduce coverage bias: ETFs with available indicator rows can receive scores, while ETFs without those rows cannot.

There is also short-history risk. Some scoreable ETFs may have too few cached rows for robust 60-day or 120-day interpretation, especially when data quality marks `insufficient_rows`. Before using the score in a strategy, scoreability must be separated from data eligibility.

## Conditions Before ETF-GAP-008B

ETF-GAP-008B should only connect an independent candidate strategy after these conditions are met:

- `factor_score_audit.csv` shows no high-severity blocking dependency for candidate-critical factors.
- `factor_score_gate.csv` has `gate_status=passed_for_candidate_research`.
- `computable_ratio` is at least 0.80 and `unable_to_score_ratio` is at most 0.20.
- Benchmark mappings and index cache are schema-valid enough for `tracking_error` and benchmark-relative returns.
- NAV or IOPV data exists before enabling `discount_premium`.
- Metadata coverage is high enough before enabling `fund_size` or `management_fee`.
- Short-history and data-quality gates are applied before any scoreable ETF becomes a candidate.
- The candidate output remains independent from `compare_signal` until explicitly reviewed.

Until then, factor score output should remain a research and audit artifact.

## Factor Score Gate

ETF-GAP-008A-FIX adds `output/factor_score_gate.csv`. This file is the explicit pre-candidate decision layer. It does not generate candidates and does not change strategy selection.

The gate status is derived from row-level gate items:

- `blocked_for_strategy_use`: one or more blocking gate items failed.
- `warning_observation_only`: no blocking item failed, but some warning item still failed.
- `passed_for_candidate_research`: all gate items passed.

Current output should remain `blocked_for_strategy_use`. The blocking reasons are:

- score coverage is too low: 24 of 50 ETFs are scoreable.
- unable-to-score coverage is too high: 26 of 50 ETFs have `no_used_factors`.
- short-history bias covers all scoreable symbols.
- benchmark-dependent factors are fully unavailable.
- NAV/IOPV-dependent discount/premium is fully unavailable.
- enabled factor coverage is too narrow for candidate construction.

Short-history bias is a candidate-strategy pollutant because it can make a newly listed or sparsely cached ETF look scoreable while its return, volatility, drawdown, and liquidity windows are not comparable with longer-history ETFs. That is a data eligibility problem, not a ranking signal.

`no_used_factors` is also not a weak score. It means the ETF has no usable enabled factor evidence. Assigning it a low score would hide missingness as preference, which is exactly what the scoring policy avoids.
